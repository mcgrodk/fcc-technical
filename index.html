Preface

This page will introduce some fundamentals of the Japanese language through basic phrases, grammar, and vocabulary. Each section listed in the sidebar will link to a vocabulary list or a grammar explanation. Read the material thoroughly, then test your knowledge with the Give It a Try! exercises.

    Upon completing this lesson, you should be able to:
    • Recite basic Japanese greetings and other useful phrases
    • Make simple sentences about your name, age, and nationality
    • Recall basic vocabulary including numbers, countries, occupations, and family members

    Prerequisites: The reader should be able to read Hiragana and Katakana to follow this lesson, as Romaji will not be used with Japanese sentences. Kanji characters will not be used in this lesson.

    See Tae Kim's Guide to Japanese for a lesson on the Hiragana and Katakana writing systems.

Japanese Greetings and Useful Phrases
    Before we dive into Japanese grammar, let's learn some basic greetings and everyday phrases. You may have heard some of these before in Japanese media and pop culture. If so, you're coming into Japanese with a head start! Look over these phrases and try saying them a few times before going to the first grammar lessons.
  • おはようございます。= Good morning.
  • おはよう。= Good morning. (casual)
  • こんにちは。 * = Good afternoon. / Hello.
  • こんばんは。* = Good evening.
  • おやすみなさい。= Good night.
  • おやすみ。= Good night. (casual)
  • さようなら。= Goodbye. (has the nuance of a prolonged farewell)
  • じゃあね。= Bye. / See you later. (casual)
  • すみません。= Excuse me.
  • ごめんなさい。= I'm sorry.
  • ごめん。= Sorry. (casual)
  • ありがとうございます。= Thank you.
  • ありがとう。= Thanks. (casual)
  • はじめまして。= How do you do? / Nice to meet you. (Said as a greeting when meeting someone new)
  • よろしくおねがいします。 = Nice to meet you. (Said when introducing oneself following one's name; also used as a greeting/polite phrase in other situations)
  • おなまえは？* = What's your name?
  • はい。/ いいえ。= Yes / No

    • * The は at the end of these phrases is pronounced like わ.

    Grammar: “I Am” - NounはNounです

    The grammar particle は marks the subject of a sentence, such as “I,” “He,” “Tom,” or whatever else a speaker is talking about. The Japanese language contains several of these “particles” that show the role of a word in a sentence, and the subject marker は is the most fundamental of these particles. In learning Hiragana, you probably learned that は is pronounced like わ when used as the subject marker. This irregular reading may seem jarring at first, but will be easy to pick up as we read Japanese sentences in context.

    The word です is used to complete a sentence following a noun. です is like a period at the end of a sentence that makes something 'what it is.' It's the difference between just saying the word “doctor” and stating “I am a doctor.” By putting some nouns in their proper places with は and です, we can make some simple sentences that mean “____ is _____.”

    わたしはトムです。 I am Tom. かのじょはせんせいです。 She is a teacher.

    Note: Null-Subject Language

    Japanese is what is called a “null-subject language,” which means that the subject of a sentence can be omitted in cases when it is obvious to the speaker and listener. For example, if a person named Yuki introduces himself, ゆうきです rather than
    わたしはゆうきです is still a correct way to say “I am Yuki” because it is clear that Yuki is speaking of himself and giving his name.

    • Grammar: Are You? Is She? - The か Question Marker

    Let's add another grammar particle to what we know. The か particle marks a question, so changing our sentence ender です to
    ですか takes us from “It is ___” to “Is it ___?” Forming questions is very easy in Japanese, because we can take the structure of an ordinary statement and add か to the end to make it a question.

    がくせいですか。 Are you a student? アメリカじんですか。 Are you (an) American?
    Vocabulary: Countries and Nationalities

    Below is a list of country names in Japanese. Note that Japan and the East Asian countries are written in Hiragana (they have kanji characters as well), while the western nations are written in Katakana.

    • にほん = Japan
    • ちゅうごく = China
    • かんこく = Korea
    • アメリカ = America; the United States
    • カナダ = Canada
    • メキシコ = Mexico
    • スペイン = Spain
    • フランス = France
    • イタリア = Italy
    • ポルトガル = Portugal
    • ブラジル = Brazil
    • イギリス = Britain; the United Kingdom
    • オーストラリア = Australia
    • ドイツ = Germany
    • コロンビア = Colombia

    To turn a country name into a nationality, simply add じん (meaning “person”) to the end of it. にほんじん means “Japanese” (a Japanese person), メキシコじん means “Mexican” (Mexican person), and so on. Try this out with a few more nationalities and refer back to this country list as we use these words in the grammar lessons that follow.

    Vocabulary: Numbers and Ages

    Now let's practice reading numbers in Japanese. The numbers one through twenty are provided here, followed by the next few multiples of ten, as you should be able to grasp the pattern of the numbers after twenty. Note that the numbers four and seven have multiple readings. The readings of these numbers are usually interchangeable. The alternative pronunciations よん (four) and なな (seven) are based in cultural superstitions around the "shi" sound in these numbers, which sound similar to the Japanese word for “death.” (See Wikipedia for more on this superstition).

    Age: さい

    To say someone's age, simply take a number and add the suffix さい (“years old”) to the end. ろくさい means “six years old,”
    よんじゅうさい means “forty years old,” and so on. A notable exception is twenty years old (the age of adulthood in Japan), which reads as はたち without the さい suffix.

    To ask for an age using the か suffix, use this phrase: なんさいですか。

    An example of this question-and-answer exchange:

    なんさいですか。 How old are you? ２５さいです。 I am twenty-five years old.

    • Grammar: My, Hers, His - The の Possessive Marker

    The particle の is used between two nouns to indicate that the second noun “belongs” to the first. In context, the の particle can be used to show that something is “my” or “his” or “of” a certain group, or it has the role of the “apostrophe-s” in “John's mother.” The particle の can also be used to group many other things in relation to one another, such as a combination of city name + country.

    わたしのいえ My house わたしのおかあさんはせんせいです。 My mother is a teacher. カナダのトロント Toronto, Canada (note that the country name comes first in Japanese, as in “Canada's Toronto” or “Toronto of Canada.”
