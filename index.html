Japanese Greetings and Useful Phrases

Before we dive into Japanese grammar, let's learn some basic greetings and everyday phrases. You may have heard some of these before in Japanese media and pop culture. If so, you’re coming into Japanese with a head start! Look over these phrases and try saying them a few times before going to the first grammar lessons.

Grammar: “I Am” - NounはNounです

The grammar particle 「は」marks the subject of a sentence, such as “I,” “He,” “Tom,” or whatever else a speaker is talking about. The Japanese language contains several of these “particles” that show the role of a word in a sentence, and the subject marker 「は」is the most fundamental of these particles. In learning Hiragana, you probably learned that 「は」is pronounced like 「わ」when used as the subject marker. This irregular reading may seem jarring at first, but will be easy to pick up as we read Japanese sentences in context.

The word 「です」is used to complete a sentence following a noun. 「です」is like a period at the end of a sentence that makes something 'what it is.' It's the difference between just saying the word “doctor” and stating “I am a doctor.” By putting some nouns in their proper places with 「は」and 「です」, we can make some simple sentences that say “____ is _____.”

わたしはトムです。 I am Tom. かのじょはせんせいです。 She is a teacher.

Note: Null-Subject Language

Japanese is what is called a “null-subject language,” which means that the subject of a sentence can be omitted in cases when it is obvious to the speaker and listener. For example, if a person named Yuki introduces himself, 「ゆうきです」rather than 「わたしはゆうきです」is still a correct way to say “I am Yuki” because it is clear that Yuki is speaking of himself and giving his name.

    • Grammar: Are You? Is She? - The 「か」Question Marker

    Let’s add another grammar particle to what we know. The 「か」particle marks a question, so changing our sentence ender 「です」to 「ですか」takes us from “It is ___” to “Is it ___?” Forming questions is very easy in Japanese, because we can take the structure of an ordinary statement and add 「か」to the end to make it a question.

    がくせいですか。 Are you a student? アメリカじんですか。 Are you (an) American?

    Below is a list of country names in Japanese. Note that Japan and the East Asian countries are written in Hiragana (they have kanji characters as well), while the western nations are written in Katakana.

    にほん = Japan ちゅうごく = China かんこく = Korea アメリカ = America/ the U.S. カナダ = Canada メキシコ = Mexico スペイン = Spainフランス = France イタリア = Italy ポルトガル = Portugal ブラジル = Brazil イギリス = Britain / the UK オーストラリア = Australia ドイツ = Germany コロンビア = Colombia

    To turn a country name into a nationality, simply add 「じん」(meaning “person”) to the end of it. 「にほんじん」means “Japanese” (a Japanese person), メキシコじん means “Mexican” (Mexican person), and so on. Try this out with a few more nationalities and refer back to this country list as we use these words in the grammar lessons that follow.

    Vocabulary: Numbers and Ages

    Now let’s practice reading numbers in Japanese. The numbers one through twenty are provided here, followed by the next few multiples of ten, as you should be able to grasp the pattern of these numbers. Note that the numbers four and seven have multiple readings. The readings of these numbers are usually interchangeable. The alternative pronunciations よん (four) and なな (seven) have to do with cultural superstitions about the sound shi in these numbers, which sound similar to the Japanese word for “death.” (See this Wikipedia article if you’re curious about this superstition). Look over these numbers and practice reading them aloud.

    1 (いち) 2 (に) 3 (さん) 4 (し / よん) 5 (ご) 6 (ろく) 7 (しち / なな) 8 (はち) 9 (きゅう) 10 (じゅう) 11 (じゅういち) 12 (じゅうに) 13 (じゅうさん) 14 (じゅうよん) 15 (じゅうご) 16 (じゅうろく) 17 (じゅうなな) 18 (じゅうはち) 19 (じゅうきゅう) 20 (にじゅう) 30 (さんじゅう) 40 (よんじゅう) 50 (ごじゅう) 60 (ろくじゅう) 70 (ななじゅう) 80 (はちじゅう) 90 (きゅうじゅう) 100 (ひゃく)

    Age: ~さい

    To say someone’s age, simply take a number and add the suffix 「さい」(“years old”) to the end. 「ろくさい」means “six years old,” 「よんじゅうさい」means “forty years old,” and so on. A notable exception is twenty years old (the age of adulthood in Japan), which reads as 「はたち」without the 「さい」suffix.

    To ask for an age using the 「か」suffix, use this phrase: 「なんさいですか。」

    An example of this question-and-answer exchange:

    「なんさいですか。」(How old are you?) 「２５さいです。」(I am twenty-five years old.)

    • Grammar: My, Hers, His - The 「の」Possessive Marker

    The particle 「の」is used between two nouns to indicate that the second noun “belongs” to the first. In context, the 「の」particle can be used to show that something is “my” or “his” or “of” a certain group, or it has the role of the “apostrophe-s” in “John’s mother.” The particle 「の」can also be used to group many other things in relation to one another, such as a combination of city name + country.

    わたしのいえ My house わたしのおかあさんはせんせいです。 My mother is a teacher. カナダのトロント Toronto, Canada (note that the country name comes first in Japanese, as in “Canada’s Toronto” or “Toronto of Canada.”
